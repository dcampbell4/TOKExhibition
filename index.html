TOK Exhibition Development Guide

Connect abstract TOK concepts to specific, real-world objects.

Step 1.1: Choose Your Core Theme and Prompt

You must ground your exhibition in one of the following TOK themes. Choosing one helps narrow your focus and ensures TOK relevance:

Core Themes & Sample KQs

  • Knowledge and the Knower: *What motivates the pursuit of knowledge?*
  • Knowledge and Language: *To what extent does language allow us to make our private experiences public?*
  • Knowledge and Technology: *In what sense, if any, can a machine be said to know something?*
  • Knowledge and Politics: *How might knowledge reflect or perpetuate existing power structures?*
  • Knowledge and Religion: *Is faith a prerequisite for religious knowledge?*
  • Knowledge and Indigenous Societies: *What role do objects and artifacts play in the construction and sharing of knowledge?*

Step 1.2: Select an IA Prompt

  1. Select an IA Prompt:

    Review the list of 35 IA prompts below. CRITICAL RULE: Select one prompt exactly as written. Do not change a single word.

    **Tip:** A good prompt will allow you to explore a variety of perspectives and knowledge problems, not just provide a simple answer (e.g., "What makes a good explanation?").

  • 1. What counts as knowledge?
  • 2. Are some types of knowledge more useful than others?
  • 3. What features of knowledge have an impact on its reliability?
  • 4. On what grounds might we doubt a claim?
  • 5. What counts as good evidence for a claim?
  • 6. How does the way that we organize or classify knowledge affect what we know?
  • 7. What are the implications of having, or not having, knowledge?
  • 8. To what extent is certainty attainable?
  • 9. Are some types of knowledge less open to interpretation than others?
  • 10. What challenges are raised by the dissemination and/or communication of knowledge?
  • 11. Can new knowledge change established values or beliefs?
  • 12. Is bias inevitable in the production of knowledge?
  • 13. How can we know that current knowledge is an improvement upon past knowledge?
  • 14. Does some knowledge belong only to particular communities of knowers?
  • 15. What constraints are there on the pursuit of knowledge?
  • 16. Should some knowledge not be sought on ethical grounds?
  • 17. Why do we seek knowledge?
  • 18. Are some things unknowable?
  • 19. What counts as a good justification for a claim?
  • 20. What is the relationship between personal experience and knowledge?
  • 21. What is the relationship between knowledge and culture?
  • 22. What role do experts play in influencing our consumption or acquisition of knowledge?
  • 23. How important are the material tools of knowledge in shaping what we know?
  • 24. How might the context in which knowledge is presented influence whether it is accepted or rejected?
  • 25. What is the relationship between knowledge and wisdom?
  • 26. What role does imagination play in the production of knowledge?
  • 27. How does the language used to present knowledge influence how it is perceived?
  • 28. Is there a difference between having knowledge and understanding it?
  • 29. What is the relationship between knowledge and belief?
  • 30. Does all knowledge impose a responsibility?
  • 31. What is the relationship between personal and shared knowledge?
  • 32. What makes a good explanation?
  • 33. To what extent is the knowledge we produce determined by the methods of collection and analysis?
  • 34. How do metaphors and analogies influence the construction of knowledge?
  • 35. Are there limits to what we can know?

Step 1.3: Define Your Focus

Clearly articulate how you plan to interpret the prompt within your chosen theme. This will become the basis of your introduction.

Example: If the prompt is "What role do experts play...", you might define the "role" as focusing on the importance of *immersion, collaboration, and objectivity* in the process of providing knowledge. This provides a clear roadmap.

Step 2.1: Identify Three Specific, Real-World Objects

  1. Specificity is Key:

    Your object cannot be a generic image. It must be a specific item that exists in a particular time and place.

    Good Example: *The dry-erase board used by Coach Smith during the 2024 regional championship game.*

  2. Real-World Context:

    For each object, clearly state its specific real-world context and where you encountered it. This is a top-band requirement in the rubric.

  3. Thematic Connection:

    Ensure your three objects are distinct but clearly relate to the chosen IA prompt AND the TOK theme. They should offer three different perspectives or facets of the same core knowledge problem.

Step 2.2: Prepare Your Object Visuals

  • Image: Get a high-quality image of the object.

  • Citation: Provide a reference for where the image was sourced.

  • Context Label: Write a brief, specific caption identifying the object and its real-world context (e.g., Object 1: Van de Graaff generator in Museu Catavento, São Paulo, Brazil).

Your commentary is a **950-word maximum** essay that articulates a clear argument.

Step 3.1: Write the Introduction (approx. 100 words)

  • State the IA prompt exactly.
  • State the TOK theme you are addressing (e.g., Knowledge and Politics).
  • Briefly outline the main focus or interpretation of the prompt.

Step 3.2: Write the Three Object Justifications (approx. 250-300 words each)

  1. Describe the Object & Context: Briefly introduce the image and its specific real-world context.

  2. Establish the Link: Explicitly and clearly state the link between the specific object and the selected IA prompt.

  3. Develop the TOK Argument: Use TOK concepts (e.g., **justification, evidence, certainty, perspective, interpretation, language, values**) to explain *how* this object sheds light on the prompt.

    **Focus:** The commentary must be an *explanation* of the knowledge question, not just a description of the object.

  4. Support with Evidence/References: All arguments must be supported by appropriate evidence and direct, explicit references back to the wording of the IA prompt.

Step 3.3: Write the Conclusion (Omitted in the final submission)

Note: The final 950-word exhibition **does not have a separate conclusion**. The final object justification simply ends the argument. Ensure the argument feels complete by the end of the third object.

Use the `ToK Exhibition Rubric` to check your work against the highest band (9–10). Every point below is mandatory for a top score.

Rubric Checkpoint Your Exhibition Must... Self-Check
Objects & Context Clearly identify three objects and their specific real-world contexts.
Links Clearly and *well-explain* the links between each object and the IA prompt.
Justification Provide a strong justification of the particular *contribution* each object makes to the overall argument.
TOK Language Be lucid, precise, and focused, using TOK concepts to develop the argument.
Word Count Be 950 words maximum (excluding captions, citations, and bibliography).

You've reached the end of the guide!

Your goal is to clearly show the examiner that you can see **Theory of Knowledge operating in the real world**.
